## 1. **Algebraic Definition**

If
[
\vec{u} = \langle u_1, u_2, \dots, u_n \rangle, \quad
\vec{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n \rangle
]

then the **dot product** is:
[
\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n
]

ðŸ‘‰ So in 2D:
[
\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2
]

ðŸ‘‰ In 3D:
[
\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + u_3 v_3
]

It always gives a **scalar** (a single number), not another vector.

---

## 2. **Geometric Definition**

The dot product also equals:
[
\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}| , |\vec{v}| \cos\theta
]

where:

* (|\vec{u}|) = magnitude of (\vec{u})
* (|\vec{v}|) = magnitude of (\vec{v})
* (\theta) = angle between the vectors

---

## 3. **Connection Between Algebra and Geometry**

The algebraic form (sum of products of components) **matches** the geometric form.
This is why the dot product is very powerful: it links **numbers** (components) with **geometry** (angles).
